Regulatory Alert — Comment Deadline July 13, 2026

Three clocks are running.

The OMB's proposed rewrite of 2 CFR Part 200 which sets the foundational rules governing every federal grant converges with a NIST grants system migration and a still-missing BEAD policy notice on October 1, 2026. This tool maps what it means for $42.5 billion in broadband funding.

01 The Convergence

Three independent processes. One deadline.

Each track below operates on its own bureaucratic logic. The OMB rulemaking is a government-wide administrative reform. The NIST system migration is a Commerce Department IT modernization project. The BEAD 2RPN is a broadband-specific policy notice whose release date remains unknown. None of them required coordination to land in the same window.

03 The Ambiguity

What "grandfathered" actually means is unclear.

The proposed rule states it will not impact "existing fixed amount awards or subawards issued prior to the effective date." That sentence does less work than it appears to. Each provision below sits somewhere on a spectrum from clearly protected to clearly subject to the new rules — and the middle is wide.

Broad interpretation (only truly new awards affected) Narrow interpretation (amendments, novations, queued actions affected)

The narrow interpretation derives from the proposed rule's inclusion of "amendments" in its effective-date language. The broad interpretation assumes OMB intends to protect all actions predating October 1. Neither has been confirmed by OMB. Comments requesting clarification close July 13.

04 The Three Tracks

Each process, explained.

Track A — OMB
The largest rewrite of federal grant rules since 2013

On May 29, OMB published a proposed rule rewriting 2 CFR Part 200 — the Uniform Guidance that sits beneath every federal grant. Five provisions carry direct BEAD exposure.

Expanded termination authority. Awards can be ended if they "no longer effectuate program goals, federal agency priorities, or the national interest." BEAD GTC §44 already incorporates §200.340(a). This expansion strengthens that hook without amending any grant agreement.

FASA elimination. Fixed-amount subawards — the payment vehicle underpinning every BEAD subgrant — would be prohibited. ISPs expecting milestone payments would shift to cost-reimbursable models.

Political pre-clearance. Every new discretionary award requires a senior appointee to certify it advances the President's policy priorities. For nondeployment awards still awaiting the 2RPN, this is an additional chokepoint with no procedural clock.

Track B — NIST
The grants office that runs BEAD is changing systems in October

NTIA's grants are administered by NIST's Financial Assistance Agreements Management Office (FAAMO). Every Notice of Award, every amendment, every budget modification for every BEAD eligible entity flows through this office.

The Department of Commerce's Grants Enterprise Management Solution (GEMS) program is migrating NIST from legacy systems to the NIH eRA Federal Shared Services platform. Scheduled go-live: October 2026.

During active cutover, grant actions are typically frozen — the system cannot record what it cannot process. NOAA completed a comparable migration in FY2024 over multiple phases. NIST has not publicly announced a blackout window start date.

If NIST FAAMO cannot issue an NOA amendment during blackout, the instrument hasn't been issued — regardless of when the action was initiated.

Track C — BEAD 2RPN
$21 billion in nondeployment awards with no publication date

NTIA's Second Restructuring Policy Notice — governing $21 billion in BEAD savings — was expected in Q1 2026. It has not published as of June 3, 2026.

Every nondeployment award is a new discretionary award. Under the proposed OMB rule, each one requires senior political appointee pre-clearance certifying alignment with the President's policy priorities. There is no procedural clock on that certification. There is no requirement to explain a non-certification.

If the 2RPN publishes before October 1, nondeployment awards can be structured under the current framework. If it publishes after, those awards enter a materially different regulatory environment — DEI prohibitions, E-Verify mandates, and expanded termination authority baked in as default terms.

The timeline is not academic. 119 days separate today from October 1.

05 The FASA Problem

The payment vehicle BEAD runs on may not survive.

A Fixed Amount Sub-Award (FASA) is a grant payment structure where a subgrantee gets paid when they hit defined milestones — "network deployed to X locations," "construction complete" — rather than by submitting invoices and waiting for cost reimbursement. BEAD runs almost entirely on FASAs because NTIA obtained an OMB exception to 2 CFR §200.333 at program design.

Why FASAs matter for rural ISPs

A rural electric cooperative with a $4 million BEAD subgrant does not have $4 million in working capital. It builds the network, hits the milestone, and gets reimbursed. Under a cost-reimbursable model, it submits invoices, waits for state review, waits for federal approval, and carries the financing gap. For a co-op operating on thin margins in a high-cost area, that gap is not administrative inconvenience — it is a solvency question.

What the grandfathering clause doesn't protect

The proposed rule states it will not impact "existing fixed amount awards or subawards issued prior to the effective date." But the proposed rule also explicitly applies to "new awards and amendments issued after the effective date." That word "amendments" is not incidental.

NOA Amendments
Every budget modification, scope change, subgrantee substitution, and period of performance extension flows through an amended Notice of Award. If amendments issued after October 1 trigger the FASA prohibition, no state with an active award is fully grandfathered — because every active award will require at least one more amendment.
§200.333
Novation Agreements
When a subgrantee undergoes a merger or acquisition, a novation agreement substitutes the new legal entity. A novation executes after October 1 even if the underlying project predates it. Is that a "new" subaward? The proposed rule doesn't say.
§200.308
Unexecuted Subgrant Agreements
A subgrant agreement signed after October 1 — even for a project in an approved Final Proposal — may be a "new" fixed-amount subaward under the plain text. States mid-procurement cannot execute hundreds of complex subgrant agreements before October 1.
§200.333
System Migration Queue
Actions initiated before October 1 but not formally issued until after — because NIST FAAMO was in blackout — may not qualify as "issued prior to the effective date." The regulatory clock runs on issuance, not initiation.
NIST GEMS

These are not edge cases. They are the operational reality of a program in active execution across 56 eligible entities with thousands of subgrant agreements in various stages of negotiation, execution, and amendment.

06 The Five Provisions

What the proposed rule actually does.

Expanded termination authority
Awards can be ended if they "no longer effectuate program goals, federal agency priorities, or the national interest" — modeled on FAR termination for convenience. BEAD GTC §44 already incorporates §200.340(a). This expansion requires no GTC amendment to take effect on existing awards.
§200.340
Elimination of fixed-amount awards and subawards
OMB proposes to prohibit FASAs, finding they "can limit transparency and hinder effective oversight." The entire BEAD subgrant payment architecture rests on an OMB exception to this provision. Eliminating the vehicle mid-program forces a shift to cost-reimbursable models for all new and amended instruments.
§200.333
DEI prohibition embedded in award terms
Federal awards may not "fund, promote, encourage, subsidize, or facilitate" DEI policies — as a standard award term, not an executive order. States with equity-framed subgrant criteria or community benefit provisions face a new compliance surface. A finalized regulation is harder to enjoin than an executive order.
Award terms
Mandatory E-Verify for all recipients and subrecipients
All recipients and employees must participate in DHS E-Verify. BEAD subgrantees include rural electric co-ops, municipal utilities, Tribal enterprises, and small regional ISPs — most of whom have no federal workforce verification infrastructure. Compliance liability flows upstream to state eligible entities.
E-Verify
Political pre-clearance for new discretionary awards
Every new discretionary award requires a senior political appointee to certify it advances the President's policy priorities before issuance. No procedural clock. No requirement to explain non-certification. For nondeployment awards still pending the 2RPN, this is a pocket veto with paperwork.
§200.205(b)
07 The Action

The comment period is the only leverage point before October 1.

Public comment on a proposed rule is not symbolic. OMB must respond to substantive comments in the final rule. Well-documented comments create an administrative record that supports judicial review if the rule is challenged. States, eligible entities, subgrantees, and their counsel all have standing to comment.
